# Drive Planning Methodology

## Core Philosophy

The drive is part of the trip. How it's planned should reflect what this family enjoys and how they travel — not a one-size-fits-all template.

## Timing the Departure

### Outbound Trip

Consider these factors, weighted by what matters to this family:

1. **Traffic avoidance**: Check typical traffic patterns for the route from their specific starting point. Recommend departure windows that avoid the worst congestion.
2. **Family schedule alignment**: If the family shared info about their kids' routines (naps, wake times, etc.), use it. If they didn't, don't assume — just optimize for traffic and arrival time.
3. **Arrival time**: Work backward from a reasonable arrival time. Families generally want to arrive with enough daylight to settle in and explore. Ask if unclear.
4. **Meal timing**: Align departure with the family's natural meal rhythm, or plan stops around meals if relevant.

### Return Trip

1. **Schedule alignment**: Factor in the family's stated preferences — some families want to get home early, others prefer to squeeze in a morning activity.
2. **Traffic patterns**: Avoid peak return traffic (varies by route and day of week). Provide specific windows.
3. **Departure logistics**: Suggest packing the car the night before or during downtime to reduce morning stress.
4. **Different stops**: If possible, plan different stops than the outbound trip.

## Pit Stop Strategy

### Frequency

Adapt to the family. Do not hardcode intervals. Consider:

- **Ages of kids**: Younger kids generally need more frequent stops. But some toddlers sleep great in the car and don't need stops. Some older kids get antsy quickly. Use what the family told you, or suggest a reasonable range and let them adjust.
- **Family's stated preference**: Some families want to power through with minimal stops. Others want the drive to be an adventure with multiple discoveries. Match their energy.
- **Trip length**: For short drives (under 90 min), stops may not be needed at all. For longer drives, suggest a cadence and let the family calibrate.

### Finding Great Stops

Search for stops that match the family's interests. The right stop depends entirely on what they enjoy:

- If they love local food scenes: search for the best bakery, restaurant, or farm stand along the route
- If they love nature: search for scenic overlooks, short trails, or state parks near the highway
- If they have active kids: search for parks, playgrounds, or open spaces to burn energy
- If they want efficiency: find rest stops with good amenities and minimal detour
- If they have dietary restrictions: make sure every food stop accommodates them
- Quirky roadside attractions, general stores, or historic spots can work for anyone — but only suggest if the family's vibe supports it

**Search strategy**: `[town along route] best [thing the family likes]`, `[town along route] things to do`, `[highway corridor] stops worth making`

### What Makes a Stop Worth It

This depends on the family. Instead of a fixed scoring table, evaluate each stop against:

- **Does it match something the family said they enjoy?**
- **Is the detour acceptable for this family's tolerance?** (Some families are fine with 20 minutes; others want under 5.)
- **Does it have the basics they need?** (Bathroom access, accessibility for their kids' ages, etc.)
- **Is the duration right for their pace?** (Some families want a quick 10-minute stretch; others want a 30-minute mini-adventure.)

### Stop Presentation Format

```
## Stop [N]: [Name]
**Location**: [Town, State] — [X min detour off route]
**When to stop**: [approximate time based on departure]
**What's here**: [1-2 sentences on what makes it great FOR THIS FAMILY]
**Why you'll like it**: [Tied to their specific interests/needs]
**Bathroom**: [yes/no/notes]
**Suggested duration**: [time]
```

## Route Optimization

- Use ~~maps to compare route options. Sometimes a route that's 10 minutes longer on paper is much more scenic and has better stop options matching the family's interests
- Check for construction alerts on state DOT websites during summer months
- Note toll roads and their costs — some families prefer to avoid tolls
- Flag any sections that are winding or potentially carsickness-inducing (relevant for many kids)
- Note areas with spotty cell service and recommend downloading offline maps
- Identify the last reliable gas station before rural stretches

## Travel Kit Suggestions

Tailor these to the family:

- **Snacks**: Match to dietary restrictions and preferences. Suggest pre-portioning by leg of the drive.
- **Entertainment**: Age-appropriate for their specific kids. Ask or infer: audiobooks, music, screen time, activity books, new small toys — whatever works for this family.
- **Practical items**: Chargers, plastic bags for trash, change of clothes accessible (not buried). Add items specific to their kids' ages and needs (car seat accessories, etc.).
